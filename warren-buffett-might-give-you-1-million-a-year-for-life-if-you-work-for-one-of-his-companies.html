Warren Buffett Might Give You $1 Million A Year For Life (If You Work For One Of His Companies)

Warren Buffett is ready to do it again. He wants to set the college basketball loving world on fire by offering up a March Madness prize that no fan could turn down – $1 million a year for life.

Pretty nice, right?

Before you get too excited, it comes with a couple of conditions. It is only open to one of the 361,270 people employed at one of his many companies, and here's the really hard part: you have to have a perfect bracket up to the Sweet Sixteen. That is 48 games you have to get correctly (he mentions the first two rounds, so it is likely that he is not counting the play-in games). According to ESPN, of the 48 million brackets filled out over the last six years, only one got the first 32 right.

The virtual impossibility of the task will keep some people from bothering to enter, much like the impossible, but the lucrative challenge he posed to basketball fans a couple years ago: fill out a perfect bracket and win $1 billion. No perfect bracket has ever been reported, and the chances of filling one out are astronomical – anywhere from 1 in 4.3 billion to 1 in 9.2 quintillion.

Paul Morigi/Getty Images

With the task being pretty much impossible, many looked at the contest as a publicity ploy and nothing more. He knew no one would actually do it, so he knew he could get away with saying he'd give away $1 billion, since he wouldn't actually have to do it.

Many people blasted Buffett for dangling the impossible carrot in front of people, so this time, he is dangling the impossible carrot along with a pretty nice consolation prize. If no one succeeds in getting every game right up to the Sweet 16, whoever picks the most consecutive winners will win $100,000.

Should there be a tie, the winners will split the $100,000. So unlike the last contest, this time there will be a winner.

Some people are probably going to bash him for making the consolation prize so much less than the grand prize. They'll say that the third richest man in the world can afford to make the consolation prize $100,000 a year for life to make it comparable to the grand prize.

With a net worth of $60 billion, he certainly could afford to, but at the same time he doesn't have to offer any prize at all. So be happy he is offering the prize he is (that is, if you happen to work for him).
